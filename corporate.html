Movement Science

What Happens to the Body After 8 Hours at a Desk

Sitting is not passive. It actively degrades the body. Here is what the science shows is happening to your employees every single workday.

Eyes

Digital Eye Strain

Sustained screen focus reduces blink rate by up to 60%, causing dryness, blurred vision, and eye fatigue. Over time this contributes to chronic headaches and disrupted melatonin production.

Neck & Shoulders

Forward Head Posture

Every inch the head shifts forward adds 10 lbs of effective load to the cervical spine. Upper trapezius tension and cervicogenic headaches are among the most common desk-worker complaints.

Lower Back

Lumbar Disc Compression

Prolonged sitting increases lumbar disc pressure by 40% compared to standing. Hip flexor shortening creates anterior pelvic tilt, leading to chronic lower back pain in over 65% of office workers.

Hips

Gluteal Inhibition

Sitting shuts off the glutes via reciprocal inhibition while overactivating the hip flexors. This anterior pelvic tilt reduces hip mobility and significantly raises injury risk when employees do move.

Legs

Reduced Circulation

Static posture restricts blood flow to the lower limbs. Extended sitting deactivates calf muscles — the body's secondary heart — increasing deep vein thrombosis (DVT) risk by up to 2×.

Brain

Reduced Neuroplasticity

Physical inactivity suppresses BDNF production — the brain's growth hormone. Decision fatigue arrives earlier, cortisol spikes increase, and cognitive performance drops measurably by midday.
